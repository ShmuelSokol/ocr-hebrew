# Target customer

## Primary

**The scholar/archivist who already pays a typist.** Has a backlog of handwritten pages (often from a deceased family member or rabbi) and pays ~₪40/page for transcription. Typically:

- Male, 35–70, religious, Hebrew-first
- Owns a laptop but distrusts cloud services for sensitive texts
- Has hundreds to thousands of pages to process, in no real hurry
- Word of mouth + trusted recommendation >> web advertising
- Likes to pay upfront and know the full price

## Secondary

- **Yeshivas and institutions** transcribing chiddushei Torah from prior roshei yeshiva. Institutional budget, committee decisions, slower sales cycle but bigger order sizes.
- **Genealogy researchers** with 19th/20th-century Yiddish-inflected Hebrew family records. Small volume per customer but steady.
- **Sofrim** annotating margins. Niche, but they already value precision and have disposable income.

## Not our customer (yet)

- English/Yiddish OCR seekers — different product, different model.
- Real-time transcription use cases (live lectures, live captioning).
- Anyone who wants to push a button and walk away. Our UX requires ~5% review time; that's the point.

## What they don't tell you up front

- Many have a **specific writer in mind** — a late grandfather, a specific rabbi. They're not evaluating "OCR in general"; they want *his* handwriting to work.
- They will judge the product by its **first page**. If page 1 is rough, they won't wait for the model to improve.
- They talk to each other. One unhappy customer in a community kills 20 potential sales. One happy one brings 5.

## Implication for pricing & onboarding

- **Paid pilot on their own handwriting** before quoting a full package. See [pricing](pricing.md).
- **Handle setup ourselves** — don't expect the customer to curate training data. Ship a product that works, not a framework.
- **Phone support, not chat**. This buyer doesn't want a Zendesk.
